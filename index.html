Zero Knowledge Password Proofs Against Offline Dictionary Attacks

Fall 2016
Johns Hopkins University, Baltimore
Teammates: Gijs Van Laer, Rono Dasgupta
Advisor: Dr. Matthew D. Green

Traditional authentication systems offer ease of use but the security they provide often proves to be inadequate. Hackers use means to gain access to company servers and steal entire databases of password hashes. These hashes are then subject to offline dictionary attacks resulting in the disclosure of millions of passwords. Such password breaches have become commonplace and have caused several major companies to face major losses. Password reuse is a common practice among users and a password breach disclosing a single password on a particular service could result in a user also losing access to their other accounts. Solutions such as multi-factor authentication add some level of security but do not completely solve the problem. There is a need to move towards stronger authentication schemes that do not compromise on ease of use, both for the user and the service provider.
In this project, we implemet a novel authentication protocol library that is proven hard against offline dictionary attacks. Our protocol implements a combination of a Zero Knowledge Password Proof and a sequentially memory hard hash function. In a concrete instantiation of the protocol, we use Schnorr’s Zero Knowledge Password Proof combined with the Fiat-Shamir Heuristic for the Zero Knowledge Password Proof and scrypt for the sequentially memory hard hash function.

Exploring the Amalgamation of IPS and SSL Proxy

February 2016 - May 2016
Johns Hopkins University, Baltimore
Teammates: Harsh Chaturvedi, Rahanik Vora
Advisor: Dr. Xiangyang Li

End to end traffic encryption has always been a two edged sword. On one side it protects the data being transmitted from any sort of eavesdropping. At the same time the encrypted channel is the perfect medium for attackers to evade detection by the network based traffic analysis solutions (IDS/IPS). Sending traffic in clear is mostly not an option as the repercussions can be severe but given the increase in cyber breaches it is has become imperative for organizations to be able to look and inspect the traffic passing their firewalls. A work around this problem is to have a trusted third party to be the encryption proxy for the network. The encryption proxy is basically a man in the middle between the organization and the external network. The proxy will decrypt traffic coming from one side, inspect it for known attacks (misuse detection) or for anomalous data (anomaly detection), encrypt it and forward it to the final destination. Since the job of the encryption proxy is coupled closely with the inspection engine it made sense to couple these two together into an IDS-SSL Proxy. We are working on survey of the various IDS solutions that have been coupled with SSL Proxy. The demand in the market and the way they are deployed. We also propose to do an application of this functionality using a Wifi-Pineapple (as the SSL Proxy) and a Raspberry Pi (as the IPS).

Soteria - An application that makes walking back home after class safer.
Teammates: Gijs Van Laer, Piyush Madan, Rono Dasgupta
Prizes Won:

  • 1st Place Overall
  • Bloomberg: Easy to Use Hack that Helps a Broad Audience of People (Philanthropy, Health, Financing)
  • Bookholders: Best App/Program Using Internet Sourced Data
  • IT@JH: Be a Campus Hero

Project Description:
A recent incident involving a kidnapping attempt of a Johns Hopkins female student right across from campus inspired us four Hopkins graduate students to build this application. Soteria was named after the Greek goddess of safety and preservation from harm. The entire functionality of the application involves a web interface (to be used by any university's security control center), an iOS mobile application for students, an accompanying Pebble application for students as well as an iOS application for security personnel. The iOS application allows any university student to notify the university's security control center that they are walking back home (or to any other destination) and are willing to be tracked until they safely reach their destination. The control center can track the student's path and accordingly notifies the nearest available security personnel to keep an eye out for them. The student can use the application to immediately alert security in case of an emergency or imminent threat and can also call security or 911 at the tap of a button. The accompanying Pebble application allows any student who owns one to never have to pull out their phones to be protected by Soteria, which can prove useful in dire circumstances. Soteria has lots of room for improvement. We hope to move beyond campus security and focus on keeping entire communities safe in the future.
Technologies used: Angular.js for the web application front-end, Node.js and MongoDB for the back-end, Objective-C for the iOS application, JavaScript for the Pebble application.

Rapid Cyber-Action Plan, Policies and Response at Zero Hour of Attack

December 2015

Information technology and Internet are controlling more or less every aspect of our lives today. In coming years the dependency on IT and internet will be on a rise and this makes them a perfect target for malicious/non-malicious adversaries to attack. As a cyber-security community it is our responsibility to see to it that such attacks do not cause major damage to a company or government as an average citizen expects IT and Internet will make their lives easier and they certainly don’t want their data to be com-promised time and again. HTML Steganography and Logic Keys

April 2016

Steganography is the art and science of hiding data in plain sight. It has been around for a long time and even though it is not cryptographically secure, people use it for multiple purposes. In this paper we will look at past research done on steganography in general and discuss the major steganography methods for text, image, audio and video. We then move on to look at HTML Steganography in detail. We propose the notion of Logic Keys and a list of Logic Keys. We will see why it is important to maintain this extensive list and how it will be beneficial and speed up the process of HTML Steganography. There are many techniques researched in steganography and we will attempt to add these to the list of Logic Keys and also propose few Logic Keys of our own. We will also look at these Logic Keys from the implementation purpose as at the end of the day if a technique is only good on paper and not in real world then it is not good at all. Finally, we will discuss the possible applications of HTML Steganography and Logic Keys so that the future researchers take this paper as a foundation for their further work.

Vulnerability Testing Tool for SQL Injection Attacks

April 2014

SQL (Structured Query Language) is a common language used to insert, retrieve, update and delete data from the databases. When we enter our information in the input fields provided on the web form of a Web Application, it forms a part of an SQL query written at the a back end, to be executed on the database. An attacker can, however inject SQL code through the front end of the Web application and gain access to information in the database. This is general principle of an SQL Injection Attack which is a serious data security threat. We propose to create a security testing tool that can gind the vulnerabilities and threats that a web application is prone to, which will cover a few of the significant Injection attacks that are presently known. We will also be generating log report primarily based on the queries fired and their corresponding results.
